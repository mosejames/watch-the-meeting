What Happened

A council member discovered a $2,995 payment to "Atlanta Funeral Assistance, LLC" listed under contractual services in the general ledger. The interim city manager (Hicks) acknowledged authorizing the payment for a long-time city employee whose son died and whose family could not cover funeral costs. The city attorney warned on the record that public funds cannot legally be used for private charitable purposes, regardless of how sympathetic the circumstances.

Why It Matters

This is a textbook misuse of public funds. The city attorney's own legal opinion, delivered in real-time, confirmed it crosses a legal line. The city manager admitted to making the decision unilaterally. The payment was buried in a contractual services line item — not disclosed to council — suggesting it was not meant to be found.

Key Quotes

"We are paying for a funeral service, full stop, is an issue... We don't have the authority to do that with public money."

City Attorney confirmed that the law is strict about using public money for private purposes, regardless of the worthiness of the cause.

What Happened

A council member flagged that the city had already paid in excess of $10,000 to engineering firm Grady this fiscal year — without council approval, violating the city's own ordinance requiring authorization for expenditures over $10,000. The city manager asked the council to approve it retroactively.

Why It Matters

The "approve first, ask later" pattern was raised by multiple council members and residents as a recurring governance violation. Council member Carr stated that the city manager cannot be allowed to unilaterally make spending decisions, and that this takes the council out of the delegated oversight process.

Key Quote

"You're violating charter. That's wrong. You're violating the ordinance. It's not about not letting the public hear — it's the fact that you're violating charter."

What Happened

The finance director confirmed that $1,034.99 was paid out due to a "finance error" — an overpayment that was subsequently repaid. The error reportedly occurred under a previous city manager. This transaction was only surfaced through an open records request, not internal controls.

Why It Matters

The fact that a four-figure overpayment was only discovered through an external accountability effort — not internal auditing — is itself a finding. Council members appeared eager to explain it away, with one dismissively referencing the website that exposed it. The mayor later apologized for posting the Home Depot transaction specifically, saying full context wasn't available, but notably did NOT retract her other documented spending concerns.

What Was Said

During closing remarks, Council Member Gay made a series of statements defending the use of public relationships in city business. Referencing past investigations, he stated openly that "it ain't illegal to make a friend rich" and followed up with: "If you do business with your friends, you do it with your friends. They don't throw favor." He then referenced entities that have received "over $500 million" and asked, "You think they walked around broke?"

Why It Matters

This is an elected official, on the record, in a public meeting, making the case that enriching personal friends through city business is acceptable. In the context of a meeting where the council just spent hours debating unauthorized spending, procurement bypass, and lack of oversight — this statement reveals a mindset about public money that every taxpayer in College Park should hear for themselves. He also acknowledged that approvals weren't obtained: "We didn't get approval. The job is running."

Key Quotes

"It ain't illegal to make a friend rich and lovely enough to make you friendly. If you do business with your friends, you do it with your friends."

"We didn't get approval. The job is running."

"Look at it — it's been $100 million... good people don't like that. But that's how they move, that's the world we live in."

What Happened

The council voted to impose new restrictions on the mayor's community enhancement fund spending. The mayor objected on the record, stating that "no other person who's ever sat in this chair has ever faced any restrictions that no other member of this body is subject to" and that the restrictions "run conflict with our charter."

Why It Matters

The mayor has been publicly raising spending concerns about council members. The council responds by restricting the mayor's spending authority — while not subjecting themselves to similar scrutiny. This is selective accountability in plain view.

Key Quote

"I will object to selective accountability — rules for one office while the spending of others goes unexamined. We all have a responsibility to steward taxpayer dollars like they are our own."

What Happened

The meeting opened with an immediate procedural fight. A council member attempted to move items off the consent agenda — citing Robert's Rules of Order, which allows any single member to object. Another council member countered with a motion to block the procedural move. That motion died for lack of a second.

Why It Matters

Consent agendas are a known tool for burying controversial items so they pass without individual debate. The fight at the start of the meeting signals that some council members are trying to prevent scrutiny of specific agenda items. The fact that one member had to cite Robert's Rules to exercise a basic right shows how consolidated procedural control has become.

What Happened

Multiple speakers identified a pattern where the city manager enters into professional service agreements and then presents them to council for retroactive approval. Public commenters described this as "executed first, approve later, maybe." Council member Carr stated he doesn't believe there's been a single meeting where something hasn't already been started, paid, or completed before coming for approval.

Why It Matters

This is not a one-off — it's an admitted recurring practice. It effectively removes council oversight from procurement, which is exactly what the $10,000 ordinance threshold was designed to prevent.

Key Quote

"We can't allow the city manager to unilaterally make decisions. It takes us out of the process — the delegated process."

What Happened

A series of senior residents — some who have lived in College Park for 48 years — testified about Airbnb and short-term rental nuisances destroying their neighborhoods. They revealed that College Park has no specific ordinance governing short-term rentals, despite collecting fees from these properties. One resident asked: "How can we collect funds if we don't have guidelines in place?" The zoning hearing on this topic had been pulled from the agenda.

Why It Matters

The city is collecting revenue from unregulated activity with no enforcement mechanism. The ordinance was pulled from the agenda, delaying action further while residents suffer.
