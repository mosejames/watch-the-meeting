From Atlanta Airport District to Destination College Park: How the City's Tourism Marketing War Unfolded

A years-long fight over who controls College Park's tourism dollars culminated in a council vote to replace the regional ATL Airport District DMO with a locally run nonprofit, over unanimous public opposition and warnings of economic damage.
January 2, 2024 – June 30, 202635 meetings13 moments

For years, the City of College Park relied on the Atlanta Airport District, a regional destination marketing organization, to market its hotels, attractions, and convention business. The arrangement made geographic sense: College Park sits at the doorstep of Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport, and the ATL Airport District pooled resources from multiple jurisdictions to market the area as a single hospitality destination. But inside College Park's city council chambers, a faction grew dissatisfied. Some council members argued the city was pouring roughly $2 million a year into a regional body where College Park held only about 33% of the board seats, while the city's identity was subsumed under a broader brand. They wanted local control.

The vehicle for that local control became Destination College Park, a nonprofit founded by Karen Jeremy, a tourism consultant who had been contracted by the city for visitor center work, historical preservation, and World Cup preparation. As Jeremy's profile grew, so did the friction. Mayor Bianca Motley Broom and some residents questioned the nonprofit's capacity, its accountability for public dollars, and the $24,000 performance bonus Jeremy received. Councilwoman Jamelle McKenzie, who sponsored many of Jeremy's presentations and contracts, and Councilman Roderick Gay, who championed tourism spending, pushed hard in the other direction, framing the shift as overdue self-determination.

The showdown came on June 15, 2026, when the council voted to pass two DMO takeover ordinances despite every public speaker opposing the move. The story of how College Park got there is a story about money, control, personality clashes, and a fundamental disagreement over whether a small city should go it alone on the global stage just weeks before the FIFA World Cup arrived in metro Atlanta.

The case for a local DMO goes public: residents and council members debate the split

The March 3, 2025 session was the first time the DMO debate broke into the open. Resident Michael Lindsay submitted a public comment arguing that 'College Park should be a destination and it's looked at as a pass through,' framing the city's $2 million annual DMO investment as wasted money. From the dais, a speaker laid out the structural complaint: the original DMO arrangement 'was il out pretty well' but 'as the partners have shifted over time it looks like it's put us in a position of disadvantage where we're fing over a lot of not aot partners' and that College Park had 'about 33% board' representation despite funding a disproportionate share. Another speaker pushed for 'independent or localized DMO,' complaining that 'when I think the Atlanta District gu is cute is lovely' but 'we don't station why are we not using that.'

But the idea was not without opponents even at this early stage. A speaker identified as Godfrey offered a detailed rebuttal, saying 'I'm not in favor of us separating from the ATL District DMO' and warning of 'reduced marketing power and budget constraints.' The speaker argued that creating a separate College Park DMO would mean 'operational challenges and expertise gaps' and would 'create competitive resources and Industry support to have the ATL DMO as well as the College Park DMO um works against us.' The lines were drawn.

November 3, 2025

Karen Jeremy presents tourism spending vision with no council oversight questions

By November 2025, Karen Jeremy was operating as a de facto tourism director. She appeared at the November 3 meeting to lay out her vision, introducing herself: 'I am Karen Jeremy and I am the owner, not the owner because you can't own a nonprofit as I've said before, but I am the founder and I am the president of Destination College Park. In addition to that, I have been bestowed the honor of being the president of the College Park Historical Society.'

Jeremy went on to explain the funding streams in play: 'TCT stands for tourism convention and trade shows as well as the TPD stands for tourism product development. Both of those dollars are coming out of the hotel motel tax that visitors and tourists pay.' She outlined plans for a visitor center, a depot museum, a botanical garden, and 'creating a better destination experience for people when they come here.' She noted that 'There are already an existing partnership with the Atlanta airport district.' A finding observed that Jeremy 'used gifted public comment time to announce major spending plans, a depot museum, a botanical garden, a visitor center, without any council member asking a single question.' The same meeting saw $250,000 quietly approved on the consent agenda for a train depot renovation, 'a project publicly championed by Mayor Pro Tem McKenzie.'

April 20, 2026

Workshop erupts: Jeremy presents Destination College Park plans, refuses to explain $24K bonus, McKenzie moves to strip mayor's authority

The April 20 workshop session was the most explosive confrontation in the DMO saga to date. Karen Jeremy presented a Destination College Park update, sponsored by Councilwoman McKenzie. Jeremy carefully distinguished her role: 'I am not the official DMO. I say that every time I come up and I want to please hammer that home. We have the great um Atlanta airport district and I am not there to replace. I am doing a totally different thing with historic tourism.' She outlined her mandate: 'infrastructure preparedness,' 'opportunities for economic and business growth through tourism,' and 'resident benefit focus.' She announced plans for a historical museum opening in June, a menu translation initiative for the World Cup ('translate all the menus of all of the restaurants in English and Spanish and Haitian Creole'), and beautification projects including 'the beautiful baskets as part of the beautification park.'

The session detonated when the mayor asked Jeremy to explain how she met performance benchmarks to earn a $24,000 bonus. Jeremy's response was evasive: 'I for one know that if there are no people on a Saturday and I bring 10 people I've increased it by 100%.' She then counter-attacked, asking what the mayor had done with '$400K capital campaign funds.' Jeremy also asked pointedly: 'why was this question not asked of the Atlanta airport DMO who has the contract to increase tourism for the city of College Park by more than just 10%.'

Councilman Gay revealed he had never seen the final tourism report that supposedly justified the bonus. But rather than join the questioning, Councilwoman McKenzie exploded, accusing the mayor of 'badgering' the contractor and formally moving to 'override the chair' to prevent further questions. McKenzie separately lamented the city's absence from airport marketing: 'It pains me to walk through that airport and you know how I feel too, Margaret, and I see other cities. I see Douglas County. I see Clayton County... Why are we not there?' The session laid bare the alliance between McKenzie, Jeremy, and the push for local tourism control, while revealing that basic accountability mechanisms were treated as personal attacks.

June 15, 2026

Council rams through DMO takeover ordinances as every public speaker opposes the move

The climactic vote came on June 15, 2026. The council took up two ordinances simultaneously: one governing DMO standards and a companion resolution directing how recovered tourism funds should be spent, effectively replacing the ATL Airport District with a new local arrangement. A resident described the maneuver as 'adopting two documents simultaneously. There's an ordinance governing the DMO standards and a companion ordinance that directs how recovered tourism should be spent while also voting to replace the current DMO at nearly midnight after this meeting.' The companion resolution, the resident alleged, 'pre-allocates millions of dollars in recovered tourism funds even before the successor DMO is even selected.'

The public comment period was devastating for the council's position. Every single speaker opposed the change. Brett Riker, a council member from neighboring Hapeville, called the replacement plan 'a hastily organized 501c3 that so far appears to be crudely homemade, woefully underdeveloped, and illequipped for the scale of work currently being done exceptionally well by the ATL district.' He warned that 'quickly leaving the current DMO behind could be devastating for your local economy, the ATL district organization and their staff members, and also create irreversible economic and reputational damage areawide.' He noted that 'every single response and reaction I read was against this action in this manner at this time.'

Nicole Adams, a College Park resident who served on the CVB board, warned of immediate financial consequences: 'I have several agreements uh that are before me and College Park is not in consideration at this time although I do my due diligence as a board member because I don't know what's going to happen and I cannot make a recommendation to anyone that's going to bring 20 to $30 million in metro Atlanta and College Park.' Christy Dean, owner of Drip Coffee, called the ATL Airport District 'the one constant, dependable partner I could rely on as a business owner in this city for the past eight years' through 'all the turnover, resignations, and terminations.' Wayne Whitesides, a Ward One resident and marketing agency owner, raised conflict-of-interest concerns: 'approximately half of our tourism funds discussed appear earmarked for conservies and organizations have direct ties to members of this council.' Resident Sheri Godfrey alleged that 'an anominous social media page reportedly um connected to city contractors who administer that page, published smears targeting Miss Miller while also promoting this successor organization.' A resident also pointed out that 'a resolution co-authored by Gay pre-allocates $2M in tourism funds to an entity bearing his name.'

Despite all of this, the council passed both DMO takeover ordinances. Where it stands now

The DMO takeover ordinances passed on June 15, 2026, but the vote resolved nothing cleanly. College Park now faces the task of standing up a local tourism operation capable of replacing a regional organization with years of institutional knowledge, data infrastructure, and industry relationships, all while the FIFA World Cup bears down on metro Atlanta within weeks. Every public speaker who addressed the council opposed the change. Business owners warned of lost deals. A neighboring city's elected official called the replacement entity 'crudely homemade.' Residents alleged that tourism funds were being pre-allocated to organizations with direct ties to sitting council members. The council majority, led by McKenzie and Gay, framed the move as long-overdue self-determination for a city that had been treated as a pass-through. Mayor Motley Broom framed it as governing in silos. Whether Destination College Park can deliver on the promise, or whether the warnings of economic and reputational damage prove prescient, will be tested in real time as the world arrives at College Park's doorstep.
